People always ask about the transition from astronomy to AI. The honest answer is that it felt less like a career change and more like swapping one telescope for another. In both fields, you're building pipelines to extract signal from noise, writing code to automate tasks that would be impossible to do by hand, and spending most of your time debugging why the output doesn't match your expectations.

The skills that transferred directly: statistical thinking, comfort with uncertainty, writing Python pipelines that process terabytes of data, and the ability to read a dense paper and extract the three ideas that actually matter. What didn't transfer: the patience for 18-month publication cycles. In AI, you can ship something useful in a week. After years of waiting for telescope time allocations and peer review rounds, that immediacy was intoxicating.

What I miss most about astronomy is the sense of exploring something genuinely unknown. When I was hunting for ultra-diffuse galaxies, every new detection was a data point that could shift our understanding of how galaxies form. AI has its own frontier, but it's a different kind of unknown — less "what exists out there" and more "what can we build." Both are thrilling. I just traded the Hubble Deep Field for a terminal window.
